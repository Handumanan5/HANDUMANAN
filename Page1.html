Why are you looking at me?

This surrealist piece delves into themes of paranoia and existential dread, a poignant metaphor for internalized fear and self-judgment. The central figure, fragile and vulnerable, embodies an individual crushed by the relentless gaze of society. The exaggerated, bloodshot eyes are symbols of invasive scrutiny, representing not only societal surveillance but also the intense self-criticism that amplifies feelings of inadequacy, guilt, and shame.

The red walls and floor evoke a sense of entrapment and urgency, a visceral representation of the mental prison forged by anxiety and depression. This claustrophobic scene mirrors the modern condition of living under constant observation, whether by societal expectations or our own internal pressures.

The figure's posture, curled and seeking refuge, speaks of a desperate desire to escape or hide, yet the omnipresent eyes offer no sanctuary, underscoring the inescapability of this psychological torment. This painting stands as a poignant commentary on the pervasive anxiety of contemporary life, inviting us to reflect on our own struggles with these pressures and consider the impacts on mental and emotional well-being.

The Process

The painting, “Why are you looking at me?”, holds a special place in the group’s art gallery, as it is the first piece to be produced by the members on the 14th of February 2024, commencing an imaginative, creative, and insightful journey for the Art Appreciation course.

Alongside the contribution of art materials, the members poured out their creative juices in developing a theme for the project with a hint of personal inspirations and experiences. The main objective was to paint an artwork that would embody one of the four (4) art movements that were previously discussed by our instructor, such as the Baroque, Expressionism, Realism, and Surrealism. After brainstorming, the group chose Surrealism because aside from the agreed sentiment that such style would give us more freedom to put up elements without worrying about clashing art styles, the movement’s principle would allow us to exercise our recent insights from our major degree courses on the wonders of the unconscious mind.

Moreover, the process boiled down to the production of the piece that depicts a paranoic event of an anxious human being who thinks that all eyes are on him, watching his every deed every day. From sketching to coloring, everyone gave a hand in the successful realization of this concept, making this the initial exhibit of our team’s collaborative exchange of wit and skills.
